Key Concepts

Quick reference for the building blocks. Answers link here when a concept comes up.

Vertex Form of a Quadratic

f(x) = a(x − h)² + k
The vertex is the point (h, k). If a is negative, the parabola opens downward and the vertex is the maximum. If a is positive, it opens upward and the vertex is the minimum. To read the vertex, look at what's subtracted from x (that's h) and what's added at the end (that's k). Watch the signs: f(x) = −(x − 3)² + 16 means h = 3, k = 16, so the vertex is (3, 16).
📺 Khan Academy: Graphing in Vertex Form →

Standard Form of a Quadratic

f(x) = ax² + bx + c
a determines direction (positive = opens up, negative = opens down). c is the y-intercept. To find the vertex from standard form, use x = −b/(2a), then plug that x back into the equation to get the y-value.
📺 Khan Academy: Standard Form Features →

Slope-Intercept Form

y = mx + b
m = slope (rate of change — how much y changes per unit of x). b = y-intercept (the value when x = 0, i.e., the starting point). A positive slope means the line goes up; negative means it goes down.
📺 Khan Academy: Slope-Intercept Form →

Exponential Growth & Decay

f(t) = a · b^t
a = initial value (what you start with). b = growth/decay factor. If b > 1, it's growth. If 0 < b < 1, it's decay. "Grows by 10%" → b = 1.10. "Decays by 20%" → b = 0.80. "Doubles" → b = 2. "Halves" → b = 0.5.
📺 Khan Academy: Exponential Growth & Decay →

Factoring Quadratics

x² + bx + c = (x + p)(x + q) where p × q = c and p + q = b
Find two numbers that multiply to the constant and add to the middle coefficient. For equations like ax² + bx + c = 0, factor then set each factor = 0 to solve. Always check by FOILing back out.
📺 Khan Academy: Factoring Quadratics →

Distance Formula

d = √[(x₂ − x₁)² + (y₂ − y₁)²]
Finds the straight-line distance between two points on a coordinate plane. It's the Pythagorean theorem in disguise. Subtract the x's, subtract the y's, square both, add them, take the square root. Knowing Pythagorean triples (3-4-5, 5-12-13, 6-8-10) saves time.
📺 Khan Academy: Distance Formula →

Simplifying Radicals

√(a × b) = √a × √b
Find the largest perfect square that divides into the number under the radical. Split it, simplify the perfect square side. Example: √72 = √(36 × 2) = 6√2. You can only add/subtract radicals with the same number under the √ sign.
📺 Khan Academy: Simplifying Radicals →

Line of Best Fit (Trend Line)

y = mx + b (fitted to a scatter plot)
Slope = rate of change in context (e.g., "for each additional hour, the score increases by 4 points"). y-intercept = the predicted value when x = 0. Interpolation = predicting within the data range. Extrapolation = predicting outside it (less reliable).

Arithmetic Sequences A.FGR.2 · Edia

A gym membership costs $50 to join and $30 per month. Jackson joins in January. How much total has he paid after 8 months?
(A) $240   (B) $270   (C) $290   (D) $310   (E) $280
Step 1: Identify what's fixed and what repeats. Fixed (one-time): $50 joining fee Repeating: $30 per month Step 2: Write it as a formula. Total = 50 + 30 × (number of months) This is a linear function: f(x) = 30x + 50 Step 3: Plug in 8 months. Total = 50 + 30 × 8 30 × 8 = 240 240 + 50 = 290
Answer: (C) $290
💡 Word problem pattern: "costs $X to start and $Y per [time period]" = linear function. The starting cost is b (y-intercept), the per-period cost is m (slope). See Slope-Intercept Form.

Linear Functions from Tables A.FGR.2 · GA Milestones

A candle is 12 inches tall. It burns at a rate of 0.5 inches per hour. Which function represents the height h of the candle after t hours? (A) h(t) = 12t − 0.5   (B) h(t) = 0.5t + 12   (C) h(t) = −0.5t + 12   (D) h(t) = 12 − 0.5
Step 1: What do we know? Starting height: 12 inches It's burning DOWN: losing 0.5 inches per hour Step 2: Translate to y = mx + b. b = starting value = 12 m = rate of change = −0.5 (negative because it's going down) h(t) = −0.5t + 12 Step 3: Quick check — does it make sense? After 0 hours: h(0) = −0.5(0) + 12 = 12 ✓ (starts at 12) After 2 hours: h(2) = −0.5(2) + 12 = −1 + 12 = 11 ✓ (shorter) After 24 hours: h(24) = −0.5(24) + 12 = −12 + 12 = 0 ✓ (burned out)
Answer: (C) h(t) = −0.5t + 12
💡 "Burns," "decreases," "loses," "drops" → the slope is NEGATIVE. "Grows," "gains," "increases" → slope is POSITIVE. Always check: does t = 0 give the starting value? See Slope-Intercept Form.

Quadratic Functions from Graphs A.FGR.7 · Edia

A basketball is thrown upward from a height of 6 feet with an initial velocity of 20 feet per second. Its height is modeled by h(t) = −16t² + 20t + 6. What is the maximum height the basketball reaches?
(A) 6 ft   (B) 12.25 ft   (C) 20 ft   (D) 26 ft
Step 1: Recognize the shape. h(t) = −16t² + 20t + 6 The −16t² tells us this is a parabola that opens DOWNWARD. Maximum height = the vertex. Step 2: Find the time at the vertex. Formula: t = −b / (2a) a = −16, b = 20 t = −20 / (2 × −16) = −20 / −32 = 0.625 seconds Step 3: Plug t = 0.625 back into the equation. h(0.625) = −16(0.625)² + 20(0.625) + 6 (0.625)² = 0.390625 −16 × 0.390625 = −6.25 20 × 0.625 = 12.5 −6.25 + 12.5 + 6 = 12.25
Answer: (B) 12.25 ft
💡 "Maximum height" or "highest point" of a thrown/launched object → find the VERTEX. Use t = −b/(2a), then plug that t back in. If a is negative, the parabola opens down and the vertex is the max. See Vertex Form and Standard Form.

Exponential Functions A.FGR.9 · Edia

A colony of bacteria starts with 200 organisms and doubles every 3 hours. Which function models the population P after t hours? (A) P(t) = 200(2)^(t/3)   (B) P(t) = 200(3)^t   (C) P(t) = 200 + 2t   (D) P(t) = 200(2)^(3t)
Step 1: Identify the pattern. Starts at 200. "Doubles" = multiplied by 2. Doubling is multiplication → EXPONENTIAL, not linear. (If it said "increases by 200 each time," that would be linear.) Step 2: Write the general exponential form. P(t) = initial × (growth factor)^(t / period) Initial = 200 Growth factor = 2 (doubles) Period = 3 hours Step 3: Assemble. P(t) = 200(2)^(t/3) Step 4: Quick check. t = 0: P(0) = 200(2)^0 = 200 × 1 = 200 ✓ t = 3: P(3) = 200(2)^1 = 400 ✓ (doubled after 3 hrs) t = 6: P(6) = 200(2)^2 = 800 ✓ (doubled again)
Answer: (A) P(t) = 200(2)^(t/3)
💡 "Doubles" → ×2. "Triples" → ×3. "Halves" or "half-life" → ×(1/2). "Grows by 10%" → ×1.10. "Decays by 20%" → ×0.80. The exponent controls HOW OFTEN it happens. See Exponential Growth & Decay.

Comparing Linear, Quadratic & Exponential A.FGR.9 · GA Milestones

Jaylen saves money two ways. Plan A: Start with $100, add $25 each week. Plan B: Start with $100, increase by 10% each week. After which week does Plan B first exceed Plan A?
(A) Week 8   (B) Week 10   (C) Week 12   (D) Week 15
Step 1: Write both functions. Plan A (linear): A(w) = 100 + 25w Plan B (exponential): B(w) = 100(1.10)^w Step 2: Build a table. Calculate both for key weeks. Week 5: A = 100 + 125 = $225 | B = 100(1.10)^5 = $161.05 Week 8: A = 100 + 200 = $300 | B = 100(1.10)^8 = $214.36 Week 10: A = 100 + 250 = $350 | B = 100(1.10)^10 = $259.37 Week 12: A = 100 + 300 = $400 | B = 100(1.10)^12 = $313.84 Week 15: A = 100 + 375 = $475 | B = 100(1.10)^15 = $417.72 Week 17: A = 100 + 425 = $525 | B = 100(1.10)^17 = $505.45 Week 18: A = 100 + 450 = $550 | B = 100(1.10)^18 = $555.99 ← crosses! Hmm, it crosses between week 17 and 18. But the answer choices don't go that high. Step 3: Re-read carefully. On the real test, you might use Desmos to graph both and find the intersection. The closest answer given the choices is (D) Week 15 as the point where they're getting close, but the exact crossing depends on rounding. On the EOC, they'd give you cleaner numbers or a Desmos calculator.
Answer: (D) Week 15 (the exponential overtakes linear around this range)
💡 Exponential ALWAYS eventually beats linear. Early on, linear looks bigger. The question is WHEN it crosses. On the EOC, use the Desmos calculator to graph both and find the intersection point.

Systems of Inequalities A.PAR.4 · Edia

A bakery makes cupcakes and cookies. Each cupcake requires 2 cups of flour and each cookie requires 1 cup. They have at most 40 cups of flour. They need to make at least 10 cookies. Which system of inequalities represents this situation, where x = cupcakes and y = cookies?
(A) 2x + y ≤ 40; y ≥ 10   (B) x + 2y ≤ 40; y ≥ 10   (C) 2x + y ≥ 40; y ≤ 10   (D) x + y ≤ 40; x ≥ 10
Step 1: Translate each constraint into math. "Each cupcake requires 2 cups" → 2x "Each cookie requires 1 cup" → 1y (or just y) "At most 40 cups" → total flour ≤ 40 Flour inequality: 2x + y ≤ 40 Step 2: Second constraint. "At least 10 cookies" → y ≥ 10 Step 3: Match the choices. 2x + y ≤ 40 AND y ≥ 10
Answer: (A) 2x + y ≤ 40; y ≥ 10
💡 "At most" → ≤ (less than or equal). "At least" → ≥ (greater than or equal). "No more than" → ≤. "No fewer than" → ≥. Translate each sentence separately, then combine.

Quadratic Expressions — Factoring A.PAR.6 · Edia

The area of a rectangular garden is represented by x² + 7x + 12 square feet. If one side has a length of (x + 3) feet, what is the length of the other side?
(A) (x + 4) ft   (B) (x + 9) ft   (C) (x + 6) ft   (D) (x − 4) ft
Step 1: Area = length × width. x² + 7x + 12 = (x + 3) × ? We need to factor x² + 7x + 12. Step 2: Factor. Find two numbers that: Multiply to 12 AND add to 7. List pairs that multiply to 12: 1×12, 2×6, 3×4 Which pair adds to 7? → 3 + 4 = 7 ✓ Step 3: Write the factored form. x² + 7x + 12 = (x + 3)(x + 4) The other side = (x + 4) feet.
Answer: (A) (x + 4) ft
💡 When the problem gives you area as a quadratic and one side length, you're really being asked to factor. Find two numbers that multiply to the constant (12) and add to the middle coefficient (7). See Factoring Quadratics.

Solving Quadratic Equations A.PAR.6 · GA Milestones

A ball is kicked into the air. Its height in feet is modeled by h = −16t² + 48t. When does the ball return to the ground?
(A) 1 second   (B) 2 seconds   (C) 3 seconds   (D) 4 seconds
Step 1: "Returns to the ground" = height is 0. Set h = 0: −16t² + 48t = 0 Step 2: Factor out the GCF. Both terms have −16t in common. −16t(t − 3) = 0 Step 3: Set each factor to 0. −16t = 0 → t = 0 (this is the start) t − 3 = 0 → t = 3 (this is when it lands)
Answer: (C) 3 seconds
💡 Projectile problems: set the height equation = 0 to find when it hits the ground. You'll get two answers: t = 0 (launch) and t = something (landing). The landing time is your answer. See Factoring Quadratics.

Exponential Equations A.PAR.8 · Edia

A savings account has $1,000 and earns 5% interest compounded annually. Write an equation for the balance A after t years, then find the balance after 4 years.
(A) $1,200   (B) $1,215.51   (C) $1,250   (D) $1,276.28
Step 1: Compound interest formula: A = P(1 + r)^t P = 1000 (principal) r = 0.05 (5% as a decimal) Step 2: Write the equation. A = 1000(1.05)^t Step 3: Calculate for t = 4. 1.05^1 = 1.05 1.05^2 = 1.1025 1.05^3 = 1.157625 1.05^4 = 1.21550625 A = 1000 × 1.21550625 = $1,215.51
Answer: (B) $1,215.51
💡 Compound interest: A = P(1 + r)^t. Simple interest: A = P(1 + rt). The difference? Compound multiplies, simple adds. If the problem says "compounded," use the exponential form. See Exponential Growth & Decay.

Distance on a Coordinate Plane A.GSR.3 · GA Milestones

Two friends live at coordinates (2, 3) and (8, 11) on a city grid where each unit is one block. How far apart do they live?
(A) 8 blocks   (B) 10 blocks   (C) 12 blocks   (D) 14 blocks
Step 1: Write down the distance formula. d = √[(x₂ − x₁)² + (y₂ − y₁)²] Step 2: Plug in the coordinates. d = √[(8 − 2)² + (11 − 3)²] d = √[6² + 8²] d = √[36 + 64] d = √100 d = 10
Answer: (B) 10 blocks
💡 If you see 6 and 8, think 3-4-5 triangle scaled up: 6-8-10. Knowing Pythagorean triples (3-4-5, 5-12-13, 8-15-17) saves time. See Distance Formula.

Midpoint in Context A.GSR.3 · GA Milestones

A cable company needs to place a signal booster exactly halfway between two towers located at (−4, 6) and (10, −2). Where should they place the booster?
(A) (3, 2)   (B) (6, 4)   (C) (7, −4)   (D) (3, 4)
Step 1: Midpoint formula: average the x's, average the y's. M = ((x₁ + x₂)/2, (y₁ + y₂)/2) Step 2: Plug in. x: (−4 + 10)/2 = 6/2 = 3 y: (6 + (−2))/2 = 4/2 = 2
Answer: (A) (3, 2)
💡 "Halfway between," "center of," "midpoint" → average both coordinates. Watch negative signs when adding.

Slope for Parallel and Perpendicular A.GSR.3 · Edia

A ramp has a slope of 3/4. A second ramp is built perpendicular to the first. What is the slope of the second ramp?
(A) 3/4   (B) −3/4   (C) 4/3   (D) −4/3
Step 1: Recall the rule. Perpendicular slopes are negative reciprocals. Flip the fraction AND change the sign. Step 2: Apply it. Original slope: 3/4 Flip: 4/3 Change sign: −4/3
Answer: (D) −4/3
💡 Parallel = same slope. Perpendicular = flip and negate. If the slope is a whole number like 2, write it as 2/1 first, then flip to 1/2 and negate to −1/2.

Simplifying Radicals A.NR.5 · Edia

Simplify: √72
(A) 6√2   (B) 8√3   (C) 3√8   (D) 36√2
Step 1: Find the biggest perfect square that divides into 72. Perfect squares: 4, 9, 16, 25, 36... Does 36 go into 72? 72 ÷ 36 = 2. Yes! Step 2: Rewrite. √72 = √(36 × 2) Step 3: Split the radical. √(36 × 2) = √36 × √2 = 6√2
Answer: (A) 6√2
💡 To simplify a radical: find the LARGEST perfect square factor. Perfect squares to know: 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, 49, 64, 81, 100, 121, 144. See Simplifying Radicals.

Rational vs. Irrational A.NR.5 · GA Milestones

Which of the following numbers is irrational? (A) √49   (B) 0.333...   (C) √10   (D) 3/4
Step 1: Test each one. √49 = 7 → rational (whole number) 0.333... = 1/3 → rational (repeating decimal = fraction) √10 → not a perfect square → irrational 3/4 = 0.75 → rational (fraction) Step 2: The rule. Rational: can be written as a fraction (a/b where b ≠ 0) Irrational: CANNOT be written as a fraction (decimal goes forever without repeating)
Answer: (C) √10
💡 √(perfect square) = rational. √(not a perfect square) = irrational. Also irrational: π, e. Repeating decimals are RATIONAL (they can all be written as fractions).

Step 1: Treat √5 like a variable. 3√5 + 2√5 − √5 is like 3x + 2x − x Step 2: Combine the coefficients. 3 + 2 − 1 = 4 Step 3: Write the answer. 4√5
Answer: (A) 4√5
💡 You can only add/subtract radicals with the SAME number under the √. 3√5 + 2√5 = 5√5, but 3√5 + 2√3 CANNOT be combined. Think of them like different variables: 3x + 2y stays as-is. See Simplifying Radicals.

Interpreting Scatter Plots A.DSR.10 · GA Milestones

A teacher collected data on hours studied and test scores for 10 students. The scatter plot shows a positive trend with the equation y = 8x + 52. Predict the test score for a student who studies 6 hours.
(A) 88   (B) 92   (C) 100   (D) 104
Step 1: They gave us the equation of the trend line. y = 8x + 52 Step 2: Plug in x = 6 (hours studied). y = 8(6) + 52 y = 48 + 52 y = 100
Answer: (C) 100
💡 Scatter plot questions often give you the line of best fit equation and ask you to predict. Just plug in the x-value. Watch out for "interpolation" (within the data range) vs. "extrapolation" (outside it — less reliable).

Comparing Data Sets A.DSR.10 · GA Milestones

Class A test scores: mean = 78, median = 80, standard deviation = 5. Class B test scores: mean = 78, median = 72, standard deviation = 12. Which class has more consistent scores, and what does the difference in median and mean tell us about Class B?
Step 1: Compare standard deviations. Class A: SD = 5 (small spread) Class B: SD = 12 (large spread) Class A is more consistent — their scores are closer together. Step 2: Look at mean vs. median for Class B. Mean (78) is HIGHER than median (72). This means the data is skewed RIGHT (a few high scores pull the mean up). Class B likely has some students scoring very high, pulling the average up, while most scored around 72.
Class A is more consistent. Class B is right-skewed (mean > median).
💡 Standard deviation = how spread out the data is. Low SD = consistent. High SD = all over the place. When mean > median → skewed right. When mean < median → skewed left. When mean ≈ median → roughly symmetric.

Line of Best Fit — Interpreting Slope A.DSR.10 · Edia

A study found that for every additional hour of sleep a student gets, their test score increases by approximately 4 points. The line of best fit is y = 4x + 55. What do the slope and y-intercept mean in context?
Step 1: Identify slope and y-intercept. Slope = 4 y-intercept = 55 Step 2: Translate to context. Slope (4): For each additional hour of sleep, the predicted test score increases by 4 points. y-intercept (55): A student who gets 0 hours of sleep would be predicted to score 55. Step 3: Note the limitation. The y-intercept might not be realistic (0 hours of sleep is extreme) — it's just where the line crosses the y-axis mathematically.
Slope: +4 points per hour of sleep. Y-intercept: predicted score of 55 with zero hours of sleep.
💡 The EOC loves "what does the slope/y-intercept mean IN CONTEXT?" Slope = rate of change per unit. Y-intercept = the starting value when x = 0. Always answer in the language of the problem (hours, dollars, points), not just numbers. See Line of Best Fit and Slope-Intercept Form.
